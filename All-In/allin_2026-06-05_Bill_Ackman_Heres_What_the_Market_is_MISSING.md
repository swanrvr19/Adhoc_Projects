## Episode Summary

Bill Ackman, founder and CEO of Pershing Square, joined the All In Podcast for a wide-ranging conversation about his evolving investment philosophy, market valuations, AI's impact on business, and his ambitious plan to build a Berkshire Hathaway-like compounding machine through Howard Hughes Corporation. Ackman emphasized that his investment approach has shifted toward prioritizing long-term durable business quality and non-disruptible growth, though he remains as activist as ever—now channeling much of that energy through social media rather than traditional corporate activism.

A significant portion of the discussion focused on current market valuations, with Ackman arguing that many of the world's best businesses—specifically naming Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon—are trading at historically cheap valuations relative to their fundamentals. He drew parallels to the 2000 dot-com era, noting that investor excitement around AI-related chips, semiconductors, and energy has caused high-quality companies to be overlooked. He expressed caution about certain SaaS companies like Salesforce while being more optimistic about platform companies with low per-seat costs. On high-growth AI companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Palantir, he suggested they should be underwritten more like venture capital investments—evaluating people, opportunity, context, and deal terms—rather than traditional financial analysis.

Ackman detailed his vision for transforming Howard Hughes Corporation into a long-term compounding vehicle modeled after Berkshire Hathaway. He explained that Buffett's genius was in using insurance float to invest in equities while keeping policyholder funds in safe short-term treasuries, and that Pershing Square plans to replicate this model by redirecting Howard Hughes's real estate cash flows into an insurance operation. He noted the company trades at a significant discount to liquidation value, offering an attractive entry point for long-term investors.

The conversation also touched on the role of founder-led companies in navigating disruptive environments, the early state of enterprise AI adoption, the influence of social media personalities on market dynamics, and Ackman's famous COVID-era market calls. He acknowledged that despite widespread CEO interest in AI, actual enterprise deployment success remains limited and very early-stage.

## Key Takeaways

- **High-quality companies are undervalued**: Ackman believes Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon are trading at historically cheap multiples as investor attention has shifted to AI-adjacent plays like chips and energy.
- **AI disruption risk is at an all-time high**: The combination of unlimited compute, capital, and talent means the probability of incumbent businesses being disrupted has increased enormously.
- **Enterprise AI adoption is still very early**: Despite every Fortune 500 CEO prioritizing AI, Ackman has seen little actual success in large-scale enterprise AI deployment so far.
- **SaaS companies face existential threats**: Companies like Salesforce with monopolistic pricing on niche software products are particularly at risk; platform companies with low per-seat costs are safer.
- **High-growth AI companies should be underwritten like venture investments**: SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic, and Palantir should be evaluated on people, opportunity, context, and deal terms rather than traditional financial metrics.
- **Founder-led companies have a structural advantage**: Founders have more authority, longer time horizons, bigger economic stakes, and track records of making bold decisions that professional CEOs lack incentive to make.
- **Howard Hughes is being transformed into a Berkshire Hathaway 2.0**: Ackman plans to redirect real estate cash flows into insurance operations and invest the surplus in equities, building a long-term compounding machine.
- **Buffett's key insight was investing insurance float wisely**: Most insurance companies focus only on liabilities; Buffett focused on the asset side, which Pershing Square aims to replicate.
- **Pershing Square offers three investment vehicles**: The management company (royalty on compounding), PSUS (public vehicle trading at 18% discount to NAV), and Howard Hughes Corporation.
- **Social media has become a powerful tool for investor communication**: Ackman's direct-to-audience approach via Twitter allows him to share investment theses and build conviction among followers instantly.
